Our 12 points go to...

For more than 65 years, European countries have exchanged musical tastes and cultural quirks in Eurovision. But participation and voting in Eurovision is more than entertainment, it a political and cultural significance and provides insight to modern-day Europe.

By Laura Bejder

April 9, 2023

But Eurovision is more than just funny gimmicks. The competition might give us insight into the political and cultural makeup of modern-day Europe and the voting patterns can tell us something about special relationships between countries in Europe.

Although the contest is called Eurovision, countries from four continents have taken part throughout the years. Aside from the European countries, Morocco participated in 1980, Israel has been competing for decades, and in 2015, Australia joined the contest.

When Eurovision began in 1956, seven countries participated: Belgium, France, Germany, Italy, Luxembourg, Netherlands, and Switzerland. Gradually more countries joined – first other Western European countries and later in the 1990s and 2000s former Soviet and Yugoslavian republics entered the competition.

Countries have joined, opted out, or been excluded ever since the competition began. In 1970, Finland, Norway, Portugal, Austria, Sweden, and Denmark boycotted the show in protest of the 1969 result and the voting system.

More than 50 years later, Russia was excluded from the 2022 competition after the invasion of Ukraine in February same year. In 2023, Bulgaria, Montenegro, and North Macedonia ceased their participation – mainly due to the increased entry fee following money lost by banning Russia.

Although the inclusion and exclusion of countries might mimic larger geopolitical trends, the voting patterns are expression of cultural ties rather than political clashes. A 2005 study found that the votes express a mix of the quality of the songs and the linguistic and cultural proximity between the singer and the voting country.

One famously strong relationship exists between Greece and Cyprus. From 2002 to 2019, Cyprus awarded the maximum 12 points to Greece every year except for 2015 and 2016. In return, Greece gave Cyprus 12 points for all but two of the years they had the chance to vote for their neighbor.

In 2008, 2011, and 2013, the two countries were separated in the semifinals and Cyprus didn’t advance leaving Greece without the possibility to award them the usual points. In 2014, Cyprus didn’t participate.

The close relationship between Greece and Cyprus in Eurovision mirrors the two countries' strong connection outside. Large portions of the populations in Greece and Cyprus share a common ethnicity, language, religion, and heritage.

Sometimes – like with Cyprus and Greece – the ties are mutual. Other times, they go in only one way. One example is an exchange of votes between Turkey and Western European countries like France, Germany, Netherlands, Belgium, and Switzerland.

From 2000 until 2012, when Turkey left Eurovision, France gave Turkey a total of 170 points. However, France only received a single point back. Turkey gave them 1 point for their 2001 song Je n’ai que mon âme.

Similarly, Germany gave Turkey 161 points over the 11 years, but only got 27 points back. Researchers suggest that this might result from a significant émigré vote from countries with a sizeable Turkish minority, such as Germany, Netherlands, Belgium, France, and Austria.

Western Europe and particularly Germany experienced an influx of Turkish Gastarbeiter (guest-worker) during the 1960s and 1970s following liberal immigration polities and special "guest-worker" agreements. Today, Germany has a 2.75m-strong Turkish minority - some of whom might have been voting for Turkey in the Eurovision.
