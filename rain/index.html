Has It Always Been This Rainy?

Since the late 19th century, Denmark has become 20 percent rainier. Climate change and higher temperatures have caused wetter winters and more extreme weather in the summers - a trend that is likely to continue in the future.

Over the past century, Denmark has become rainier.

More precisely, the accumulated yearly precipitation - including rain, snow, sleet and hail - has increased by approximately 100 mm since the Danish Meteorological Institute started doing systematic measurements in 1874, according to klimatilpasning.dk.

Much of the increased precipitation falls during the fall and winter months.

In 1874-1900 the average monthly precipitation was 38.2 mm in December and 49.6 mm in January. That had increased to 57.1 mm and 65.1 mm in 1991-2020.

In general, climate change and higher temperatures will change the precipitation patterns in Denmark. According to klimatilpasning.dk, the future will bring more rainfall all year around but in particular in the winter.

Precipitation in the winter will increase by almost 25 percent and due to higher temperatures, more will fall as rain instead of snow.

On top of that, climate models estimate that cloudbursts and heavy rainfall will be more intense and occur more frequently approaching the year 2100.

During the 2010s, Copenhagen experienced several of those cloudbursts. Within five years, the city was hit by several 100-year rain events. More than 50 mm of rain within 24 hours was recorded - for the first time since DMI started measuring rainfall in the capital.

And twice the rainfall exceeded 100 mm within a day. On July 2, 2011, more than 135 mm of rain fell.
